Machu Picchu, Peru

The Lost City of the Incas

About Machu Picchu

Machu Picchu is a 15th-century Inca citadel located in the Eastern Cordillera of southern Peru on a 2,430-meter mountain ridge. Often referred to as the "Lost City of the Incas", it is the most familiar icon of Inca civilization. The site was built around 1450 but abandoned a century later at the time of the Spanish conquest.

Top Attractions

The Citadel

The main archaeological site with its impressive stone constructions built without mortar. Highlights include the Temple of the Sun, the Intihuatana stone, and the Room of the Three Windows. The site is divided into urban and agricultural sectors.

Huayna Picchu

The steep mountain that towers above Machu Picchu, offering breathtaking views of the ruins and surrounding landscape. The hike takes about 2 hours round trip and requires a separate ticket due to limited daily entries.

Inti Punku (Sun Gate)

The traditional end point of the Inca Trail, offering the first view of Machu Picchu for trekkers. It's about a 1.5-hour hike from the main site and provides panoramic views at sunrise.

Travel Tips

Best Time to Visit

April-October (dry season) offers the best weather. June-August is peak season with more crowds. The site is open year-round but February sees heavy rains.

Getting There

Most visitors take a train from Cusco or Ollantaytambo to Aguas Calientes, then a bus up to the site. The classic 4-day Inca Trail trek is another popular option.
